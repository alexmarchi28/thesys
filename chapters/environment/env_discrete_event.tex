\section{Discrete-Event Simulation for Vehicular Networks}
\label{env:discrete_event}

Discrete-event simulation (DES) is the most widely used paradigm for studying network protocols and complex distributed systems, because it represents system evolution as an ordered sequence of timed events. In this paradigm, simulated time advances from one event to the next, such as transmission, reception, timeout, or state update, thus avoiding a uniform fixed-step update of the whole system when that is not needed \cite{varga2008overview}. This approach is particularly suited to V2X systems, where very frequent network events coexist with mobility and control events evolving on different time scales.

In the case of cooperative platooning, DES is useful because it makes it possible to model explicitly the interaction among:
\begin{itemize}
\item the periodic generation of control messages and beacons;
\item channel contention and packet losses;
\item timeouts and \emph{fallback}/\emph{recovery} mechanisms;
\item updates of vehicle dynamics and controller state.
\end{itemize}
The crucial aspect is not only to simulate the network or the mobility accurately, but to preserve temporal coherence between the two domains, so as to measure the effect of communication conditions on platoon behavior.

Compared with a purely analytical model or separate simulations, with networking on one side and traffic on the other, a coupled DES makes it possible to observe emergent effects that are difficult to capture with simplified models: for instance, stability degradation due to bursts of losses, the impact of handovers on communication continuity, or feedback loops between increased safety distance and available link quality. This is precisely the type of phenomenon that motivates the use of integrated toolchains in cooperative driving and platooning research \cite{sommer2011bidirectionally,segata2014plexe,segata2023multi-technology}.

In this thesis, discrete-event simulation therefore provides the orchestration layer on top of which the network models, namely OMNeT++ with its extensions, the microscopic road mobility model in SUMO, and the platooning logic are integrated.
