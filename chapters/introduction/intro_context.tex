\section{Context: V2X and Cooperative Driving}

Cooperation among vehicles is one of the most relevant directions in the development of Intelligent Transportation Systems, because it makes it possible to overcome the limits of each vehicle's local perception and to coordinate control, safety, and efficiency decisions more tightly. Within this framework, Vehicle-to-Everything (V2X) communications play a dominant role and become an integral part of the control chain, since the informational value of a message depends on whether it arrives with timing and reliability compatible with vehicle dynamics and with the safety margins required by the application \cite{dressler2019cooperative,giordano2019joint,segata2016safe}.

Among the simplest yet most promising cooperative applications is \emph{platooning}, in which multiple vehicles travel in coordination at short distance while periodically exchanging information about their state. In this scenario, the limitations of single-technology solutions become particularly evident: congestion, interference, optical occlusions, \emph{blind spots}, and handovers can degrade the link and propagate the effects of that degradation all the way to the longitudinal controller, with direct consequences for comfort, performance, and safety \cite{segata2015communication,bazzi2020wireless,segata2023multi-technology}.

This thesis takes as its experimental reference the work of Segata \emph{et al.} \cite{segata2023multi-technology}, which proposes a multi-technology architecture and the \emph{SafeSwitch} mechanism to safely manage transitions between different levels of network reliability. The contribution of this thesis is to reproduce and update that approach on a more recent toolchain, based on updated versions of OMNeT++, Veins, and PLEXE, and to integrate Simu5G in place of the previous cellular component based on SimuLTE, in order to verify whether the approach can be transferred to an environment consistent with the current evolution of simulation tools \cite{nardini2020simu5g,virdis2014simulte}.

This choice is particularly relevant in light of the evolution of V2X technologies. The most recent C-V2X specifications indeed point toward a gradual transition from the initial LTE-based implementations to solutions built on 5G networks. Integrating Simu5G therefore makes it possible to evaluate the proposed architecture in a context that is more closely aligned with current developments in vehicular communications.
