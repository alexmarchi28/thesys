\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:intro}

The acronym V2X refers to the set of communications between a vehicle and its surroundings: other vehicles (V2V), road infrastructure (V2I), and remote services. In practical terms, these communications allow vehicles to share information that supports more coordinated and safer driving, going beyond the limits of local perception alone \cite{dressler2019cooperative,segata2016safe}.

One of the most representative scenarios is \emph{platooning}, namely the coordinated movement of multiple vehicles traveling at short distance from one another. In this configuration, a \emph{leader} vehicle guides the \emph{platoon}, while the \emph{followers} track it by combining their local sensors with data received over the network. When properly managed, platooning can improve road capacity, energy efficiency, and traffic regularity \cite{robinson2010operating,segata2016safe}.

These benefits, however, depend on communication quality: delays or packet losses can directly affect the stability and safety of the \emph{platoon}. For this reason, recent literature treats networking, control, and vehicle dynamics as parts of a single problem that must be analyzed in an integrated way \cite{giordano2019joint,segata2023multi-technology}. This thesis fits into that line of work, which is framed in greater detail in the next section.
