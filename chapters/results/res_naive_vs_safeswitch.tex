\section{Naive Fallback vs. SafeSwitch}

The first set of results aims to verify that importing the \emph{SafeSwitch} logic preserves the most important property of the reference work: preventing connectivity degradation from turning into a dynamically inconsistent transition from CACC to ACC. In the following, scenarios 1 and 3 from \cite{segata2023multi-technology} are considered in particular, because they are the ones in which the comparison with a \emph{naive} fallback is clearest. In the \emph{naive} fallback, a loss of link quality directly causes the switch from a cooperative controller to ACC, without waiting for the platoon to reach a distance compatible with the new controller. \emph{SafeSwitch}, by contrast, introduces an intermediate phase of gap adaptation, as discussed in Chapter~\ref{ch:safeswitch_5g}.

\subsection{Scenario 1: distributed failure without braking}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{scenario_1-grid.pdf}
\caption{Comparison between \emph{naive} fallback and \emph{SafeSwitch} in scenario 1. The direct fallback is shown on top, \emph{SafeSwitch} below; inter-vehicle distance is on the left and acceleration on the right.}
\label{fig:scenario1_naive_vs_safeswitch}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:scenario1_naive_vs_safeswitch} shows the case in which one technology is lost simultaneously by all followers in the platoon. Both mechanisms eventually lead to an increase in the gap toward values compatible with a more conservative behavior, but the transient is very different. In the \emph{naive} fallback, the increase in distance is imposed while the vehicles are still traveling in a configuration designed for a cooperative controller; this results in sharper accelerations and decelerations, with evident amplification toward the tail of the platoon.

\emph{SafeSwitch}, instead, reaches the same goal more gradually. The gap is increased progressively and accelerations remain more limited, which improves both comfort and the regularity of the transient. The important point is not merely aesthetic: a less aggressive transient leaves more room for subsequent disturbances and reduces the risk that local errors propagate along the platoon. In this sense, the results confirm that the \emph{gap control} phase is not an implementation detail, but a necessary element to make the controller change coherent with the physical state of the platoon.

\subsection{Scenario 3: distributed failure with emergency braking}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{scenario_3-grid.pdf}
\caption{Comparison between \emph{naive} fallback and \emph{SafeSwitch} in scenario 3, where distributed degradation is followed by emergency braking. The \emph{naive} fallback appears on top, \emph{SafeSwitch} below; inter-vehicle distance is on the left and acceleration on the right.}
\label{fig:scenario3_naive_vs_safeswitch}
\end{figure}

Scenario 3 represents the most severe case among those considered, because the distributed loss of one technology is followed by emergency braking by the vehicle in front of the platoon. Here the limit of the direct fallback clearly emerges: in the \emph{naive} case, the simulation stops almost immediately because of a collision, which indicates that the transition to ACC occurs while the vehicles are still at distances incompatible with a non-cooperative controller. The problem is therefore not only larger oscillations, but an actual loss of safety conditions.

With \emph{SafeSwitch}, by contrast, the platoon avoids the collision and progressively reaches the new driving configuration. A phenomenon of amplification toward the tail is still visible: the rearmost followers are forced into stronger braking, with peaks on the order of \(8~\si{m/s^2}\), in line with what was observed in the original work \cite{segata2023multi-technology}. This effect shows a real limitation of the mechanism, already discussed by the authors: even a correct fallback can remain dynamically demanding in extreme cases. The difference, however, is qualitative even before it is quantitative: with \emph{SafeSwitch} the transient remains controlled and the platoon stays safe, whereas the \emph{naive} fallback leads to a crash.
