\section{Realistic Scenario: Comparison with the Paper and the Role of 5G}

The realistic scenario is the most interesting one for evaluating whether the architecture can be reproduced on an updated toolchain. It reproduces the setup of the reference paper: a closed circuit with two interference zones on IEEE~802.11p and a cellular component subject to handovers \cite{segata2023multi-technology}. The purpose of the comparison is not to obtain a perfect numerical match, which is difficult to achieve after a stack migration, but to verify whether the same causal relationships among communication, fallback logic, and platoon dynamics remain recognizable.

To make the comparison easier to read, the behavior reported in the paper is shown on the left side of the figures, while the results obtained in this thesis are shown on the right.

Figures~\ref{fig:realistic_acceleration_compare} and~\ref{fig:realistic_distance_compare} show that the global behavior of the platoon remains broadly consistent between the two studies. In both cases, the loss bursts associated with the interference zones produce a temporary increase in the gap, followed by a recompression phase when communication quality becomes sufficient again. The transients related to the cellular component also remain relatively short and do not trigger permanent instabilities.

The quantitative differences, however, are evident. In the paper, some vehicles at the tail reach very large distances in the second half of the simulation, whereas in the reimplementation presented here the peaks are smaller and the return toward the nominal gap occurs earlier. Overall, acceleration levels are also less pronounced in this reimplementation. The most plausible explanation is that the transition from SimuLTE to Simu5G, together with different default values in the physical and handover models, changed the duration and temporal correlation of the losses while leaving the higher-level logic of \emph{SafeSwitch} unchanged. In other words, the mechanism still reacts to the same types of events, but the severity of the transient no longer matches point by point.

The FER curves in Figures~\ref{fig:realistic_front_fer_compare} and~\ref{fig:realistic_leader_fer_compare} help interpret the differences just discussed. IEEE~802.11p continues to show loss bursts localized in the same time windows, consistent with passage through the interference zones. The cellular component, instead, mainly exhibits short spikes concentrated around handovers, without producing prolonged losses. This behavior is compatible with the idea that handovers remain a source of transients, but do not represent the main degradation factor for the dynamics.

The comparison with the paper highlights above all a difference in VLC. In the original work, VLC FER grows sharply in the second half of the simulation, up to saturation for some vehicles; in the thesis reimplementation, this effect is much more attenuated. Since VLC is particularly sensitive to distance and line of sight, this divergence suggests that the feedback between gap increase and loss of optical connectivity, while still present as a theoretical mechanism, manifests itself less drastically in the new simulation stack. This too is consistent with the hypothesis of different radio burst severity rather than with a malfunction of the \emph{SafeSwitch} logic.

Overall, the realistic scenario suggests not only that the migration to 5G preserves the qualitative operation of the system, but also that it attenuates, at least in part, the transients related to the cellular component. Interference on 802.11p, cellular handovers, and gap dynamics remain coupled in the same causal order observed in the paper, but in the thesis results the handovers seem to translate into shorter spikes and fewer accumulated losses. One plausible explanation is that the new 5G stack manages cell transitions more smoothly, for example through more timely handover criteria or better link continuity during the transition.
