\section{Link Monitoring: PDR and Ring Buffer}
\label{sec:s5g_monitoring}

To decide when to increase or decrease inter-vehicle distances, \emph{SafeSwitch} needs a measure of link quality that is comparable across very different technologies. In the paper, this measure is the \emph{Packet Delivery Ratio} (PDR), that is, the fraction of packets correctly received within a recent time window \cite{segata2023multi-technology}. The focus is on understanding whether the information flow available to the controller is still reliable enough.

PDR estimation exploits the fact that each beacon is transmitted in parallel over the active interfaces. When a vehicle receives multiple copies of the same message over different technologies, it can compare their sequence numbers and reconstruct which packets arrived and which were instead lost on each link. In this way, losses, temporary interruptions, or quality degradation are observed not as isolated events, but as trends over time in message delivery.

To maintain this information, a \emph{ring buffer} is used, namely a circular structure that stores the outcome of the latest observed messages for each interface. At the conceptual level, the important aspect is not the data structure itself, but the fact that the system reasons over a sliding window of recent receptions rather than over a single event. This makes it possible to filter short fluctuations and evaluate link behavior more stably.

Monitoring is applied to the two information sources that are most relevant to the follower: the leader and the immediate predecessor. The statistics computed over the recent window then feed the \emph{failure} and \emph{recovery} logic. An interface is considered degraded when its PDR falls below an operational threshold; conversely, recovery requires the quality to rise above the threshold and remain there long enough to avoid oscillations caused by merely transient recoveries.

One advantage of this approach is that phenomena that are very different from one another are all mapped to the same operational abstraction. Contention on the 802.11p channel, loss of line of sight for VLC, or handover transients on the cellular network have different physical causes, but for \emph{SafeSwitch} they all take the form of a reduction in the reliability of the message flow reaching the controller.
