\section{Migration to 5G}

The migration to 5G did not consist of a simple nominal replacement of LTE with a newer technology, but in bringing into \texttt{plexe\_hetnet} the cellular architecture already developed in the \texttt{plexe\_5g} subproject. The goal was to keep the functional role of the third interface unchanged within the multi-technology system, while updating the level at which the cellular network is modeled and making it possible to replicate the paper with 5G instead of LTE.

Conceptually, Simu5G extends the architectural line of SimuLTE toward the NR world: the base station becomes a \emph{gNodeB}, the network core is represented through 5G network modules, the \emph{Binder}, \emph{handover} support, and coordination among cells remain available, and components such as \emph{carrier aggregation} are added \cite{nardini2020simu5g}. For the project, however, the most relevant difference was not so much the presence of all the general Simu5G features, but rather the availability of an NR stack already integrated with PLEXE and suitable for V2X communications.

In the new setup, vehicular communication over the cellular component was treated as 5G C-V2X \emph{Mode 1}. Here too, the logic remains coherent with the idea of communication under coverage and assisted by the network: vehicles do not operate as fully autonomous nodes in radio resource management, but rely on the NR infrastructure. From the point of view of the multi-technology system, this makes it possible to preserve the nature of the third interface as a managed cellular channel, while letting radio behavior and network transients be those of the 5G stack modeled in Simu5G.

The actual transition therefore required a complete architectural replacement of the cellular part of \texttt{plexe\_hetnet}. At the network level, the original topology based on \emph{eNodeB}s and a simplified LTE core was replaced with the corresponding NR topology of Simu5G, introducing two \emph{gNodeB}s, the \emph{UPF} node, the \emph{Binder}, IP configuration modules, and support for \emph{carrier aggregation}. The elements necessary for vehicle mobility were also preserved, such as dynamic cell association, X2 connections between base stations, and \emph{handover} management, because even in the new scenario the continuity of cellular connectivity remains part of the experimental behavior.

At the level of the single vehicle, the previous LTE interface was replaced with an NR NIC compatible with Simu5G, and cooperative communication was routed through the 5G driver already available in \texttt{plexe\_5g}. This step was important because it made it possible to reuse radio logic already designed for platooning, rather than introducing a generic 5G stack disconnected from the rest of PLEXE. As a consequence, cooperative beacons continue to be handled as one-to-many traffic on the cellular component, but they are now transmitted over the new NR interface.

The experimental configuration also required realignment: cell association parameters, \emph{handover}, transmission power, preconfigured CQI, and D2D/sidelink settings were translated into the semantics of Simu5G. In this sense, the migration was not merely a change in software dependency, but an actual translation of the system's third channel from an LTE C-V2X \emph{Mode 3} architecture to a 5G C-V2X \emph{Mode 1} architecture, while keeping the higher-level redundancy logic, PDR monitoring, and \emph{SafeSwitch} unchanged. It is precisely this step that makes the experimental contribution different from a simple reproduction of the original work: the paper's multi-technology structure is preserved, but its cellular component is updated to a 5G platform that is effectively integrated into \texttt{plexe\_hetnet}.
